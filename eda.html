Scatter Plot for Price Vs SqftLot¶

In [65]:
#Create a scatter plot including price that also maps a third variable to either the color or size of the data markers
sns.scatterplot(
    data=df,
    x='sqft_lot',
    y='price',
    hue='floors',  
    palette='viridis',
    alpha=0.8
)

plt.title("Price vs Sqft Lot")
plt.xlabel("Sqft Lot")
plt.ylabel("Price")
plt.legend(title="Floors")
plt.show()

The scatter plot reveals that there is no strong linear correlation between a property's lot size and its price, with the vast majority of homes clustered tightly at smaller lot sizes and moderate prices. Notably, the most expensive properties in the dataset possess very small lots, suggesting they are situated in premium, highly desirable urban areas, whereas properties with massive lots remain relatively low in price, indicating rural or agricultural locations. Furthermore, while 1 and 2 story homes dominate the dataset overall, the high-priced urban homes are frequently 2 to 2.5 stories, and the rarer 3+ story homes are generally confined to smaller lots. Ultimately, the visualization underscores that location and land scarcity play a far more significant role in driving property values than sheer acreage alone.

#facet plot

g = sns.FacetGrid(df, col="bedrooms", col_wrap=4, height=3)
g.map(sns.histplot, "price", bins=30)
g.figure.suptitle("Price Distribution by Number of Bedrooms", y=1.02)
g.set_axis_labels("Price", "Count")
plt.show()

The faceted histograms reveal that the vast majority of properties in this dataset are 3 and 4 bedroom homes, which both exhibit heavily right-skewed distributions—meaning most properties cluster at lower price points while a long tail of luxury outliers stretches to the higher end. As the bedroom count increases from 2 to 5, the price variance visibly expands, demonstrating that larger homes generally command higher and more widely ranging prices. Conversely, homes at the extremes (0–1 or 6+ bedrooms) represent a microscopic fraction of the market and are exceptionally rare. Most notably, the chart exposes a severe data anomaly with a 33-bedroom property, which is almost certainly a data entry typo or a misclassified commercial building that requires cleaning before conducting further analysis.

#Time Series Plot (Homes Sold by Month)

# Convert date column to datetime
df['date'] = pd.to_datetime(df['date'])

# Extract year-month
df['year_month'] = df['date'].dt.to_period('M')

# Group by month
sales_pm = df.groupby('year_month').size().reset_index(name='count')

# Convert to timestamp
sales_pm['year_month'] = sales_pm['year_month'].dt.to_timestamp()

print(sales_pm)

plt.figure(figsize=(12,6))
plt.plot(sales_pm['year_month'], sales_pm['count'], marker='o', label='Sales')
plt.title("Number of Homes Sold Per Month")
plt.xlabel("Month")
plt.ylabel("Number of Homes Sold")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

The line graph illustrating the number of homes sold per month between May 2014 and May 2015 reveals a distinct seasonal trend in the real estate market. Sales volumes demonstrate strong peaks during the spring and early summer, reaching their highest points in June and July of 2014, and again in April 2015, with both periods exceeding 2,200 homes sold. Conversely, market activity experiences a significant cooling period throughout the fall and winter, ultimately hitting a trough in January 2015 where sales drop below 1,000. While there is a drastic plummet recorded at the very end of the timeline in May 2015, this sharp decline most likely represents an incomplete dataset for that final month rather than a genuine, sudden market crash, especially given the strong upward trajectory established in the preceding spring.

Impact of Waterfront on price¶

In [74]:
#how price is impacted by Waterfront

plt.figure(figsize=(8,6))

sns.boxplot(data=df, x='waterfront', y='price')

plt.title("Impact of Waterfront Property on Price")
plt.xlabel("Waterfront (0 = No, 1 = Yes)")
plt.ylabel("Price")
plt.show()

The box plot shows a clear price difference between waterfront and non-waterfront properties.

Waterfront homes (value = 1) have a significantly higher median price compared to non-waterfront homes (value = 0), indicating a strong positive impact of waterfront location on property value.

The middle 50% of waterfront home prices (interquartile range) is much higher and more spread out, typically ranging from approximately $800,000 to over $2,000,000.

In contrast, most non-waterfront homes are concentrated at the lower end of the price distribution, generally below $1,000,000.

However, despite this overall trend, the non-waterfront category still includes several high-value outliers, with some properties reaching nearly $8,000,000. This suggests that while waterfront location strongly increases typical home prices, the most expensive luxury properties in the dataset are not exclusively waterfront.
